Small Group Discussion Notes

Endure by seeing struggle as God's training

Hebrews 12:4–17 · Dan Allen · CrossWay Milwaukee

Scripture — Hebrews 12:4–17 (ESV)

4 In your struggle against sin you have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood. 5 And have you forgotten the exhortation that addresses you as sons? "My son, do not regard lightly the discipline of the Lord, nor be weary when reproved by him. 6 For the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and chastises every son whom he receives."

7 It is for discipline that you have to endure. God is treating you as sons. For what son is there whom his father does not discipline? 8 If you are left without discipline, in which all have participated, then you are illegitimate children and not sons. 9 Besides this, we have had earthly fathers who disciplined us and we respected them. Shall we not much more be subject to the Father of spirits and live? 10 For they disciplined us for a short time as it seemed best to them, but he disciplines us for our good, that we may share his holiness. 11 For the moment all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it.

12 Therefore lift your drooping hands and strengthen your weak knees, 13 and make straight paths for your feet, so that what is lame may not be put out of joint but rather be healed. 14 Strive for peace with everyone, and for the holiness without which no one will see the Lord. 15 See to it that no one fails to obtain the grace of God; that no "root of bitterness" springs up and causes trouble, and by it many become defiled; 16 that no one is sexually immoral or unholy like Esau, who sold his birthright for a single meal. 17 For you know that afterward, when he desired to inherit the blessing, he was rejected, for he found no chance to repent, though he sought it with tears.

Context

  • The letter's audience is weary and tempted to slow down — the author has already called them to run steadfastly, run lightly, and stay Christ-focused (vv. 1–3).
  • Here the argument deepens: the struggle itself is not the problem — forgetting who God is to us is the problem.

Passage summary

  • Verses 4–6 diagnose the problem: readers have forgotten they are sons, and so they misread hardship as God's displeasure.
  • Verses 7–8 establish the norm: every son is trained; discipline is evidence of belonging, not rejection.
  • Verses 9–11 move from earthly fathers to the perfect Father — his training is purposeful, producing holiness and the fruit of righteousness.
  • Verses 12–17 call the church to run forward in light of this — not coast or trade it away like Esau.

Main points

Remember who you are: you are a son, not a stranger

Gospel amnesia — forgetting our identity as God's children — is what causes us to misread struggle as punishment or pointless pain.

  • Justification clears our record before God; adoption goes further — the judge comes off the bench and brings us home into his family.
  • J.I. Packer: the richest answer to "what is a Christian?" is that a Christian is one who has God as Father — the climax of the whole Bible.
  • A good father only and always does what is best for his child; God as Father means nothing enters your life that is not ultimately for your good.

See struggle as a sign of love, not displeasure

God trains every one of his children — discipline is not a mark of rejection but proof of belonging and love.

  • Verse 6 hits the note: the Lord disciplines the one he loves — every son, all of them, no exceptions.
  • A father who never corrects or trains a child reveals he doesn't actually care — the absence of discipline would signal we are not truly his.
  • The two wrong lenses: reading struggle as God's displeasure, or as pointless pain blocking our comfort — both miss the Father's hand behind it.

Trust that purposeful pain leads to long-term good

God's training is not pleasant in the moment — the author admits that freely — but it is never pointless; it yields holiness and the fruit of righteousness.

  • Earthly fathers train imperfectly and briefly; God trains with full knowledge of who we are and what we need, always for our good (v. 10).
  • Corrie ten Boom's tapestry: from our side the threads look knotted and chaotic; from God's side he is weaving something beautiful.
  • The fruit of righteousness only comes to those who are trained by it — there is no shortcut around the struggle to the harvest.

Run toward the struggle, not away from it

Once we have the right category for suffering, we have every reason to keep running hard — and no reason to coast or trade our future for momentary relief.

  • Verses 12–13 issue a direct command: lift your drooping hands, keep running — the training is God's pathway to victory, not an obstacle to it.
  • Esau is the counter-example: he traded everything to come for one moment of immediate pleasure.
  • Christ himself learned obedience through suffering (Heb. 5) — the path he blazed is the same path he invites us to walk, sealed by his blood.

Key idea

Struggle is not a sign that God has forgotten you — it is the very means your Father uses to train you as his beloved child and move you toward the fruit of righteousness.

Discussion questions

1

When you're in the middle of something hard, which wrong lens do you tend to reach for first — "God must be displeased with me" or "this is just pointless pain blocking what I want"? What does that reveal about how you actually see God?

2

Looking back, can you identify a season of struggle that you now recognize as God's training? What did it produce in you that wouldn't have come any other way?

3

Is there something hard in your life right now that you've been trying to escape or coast through? How might viewing it as your Father's purposeful training change how you engage with it this week?

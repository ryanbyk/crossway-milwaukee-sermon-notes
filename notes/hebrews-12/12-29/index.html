Small Group Discussion Notes

Pursue peace and purity, empowered by the unshakable kingdom

Hebrews 12:12–29 · Dan Allen · CrossWay Milwaukee

Scripture — Hebrews 12:12–29 (ESV)

12 Therefore lift your drooping hands and strengthen your weak knees, 13 and make straight paths for your feet, so that what is lame may not be put out of joint but rather be healed. 14 Strive for peace with everyone, and for the holiness without which no one will see the Lord. 15 See to it that no one fails to obtain the grace of God; that no "root of bitterness" springs up and causes trouble, and by it many become defiled; 16 that no one is sexually immoral or unholy like Esau, who sold his birthright for a single meal. 17 For you know that afterward, when he desired to inherit the blessing, he was rejected, for he found no chance to repent, though he sought it with tears.

18 For you have not come to what may be touched, a blazing fire and darkness and gloom and a tempest 19 and the sound of a trumpet and a voice whose words made the hearers beg that no further messages be spoken to them. 20 For they could not endure the order that was given, "If even a beast touches the mountain, it shall be stoned." 21 Indeed, so terrifying was the sight that Moses said, "I tremble with fear." 22 But you have come to Mount Zion and to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem, and to innumerable angels in festal gathering, 23 and to the assembly of the firstborn who are enrolled in heaven, and to God, the judge of all, and to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, 24 and to Jesus, the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel.

25 See that you do not refuse him who is speaking. For if they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, much less will we escape if we reject him who warns from heaven. 26 At that time his voice shook the earth, but now he has promised, "Yet once more I will shake not only the earth but also the heavens." 27 This phrase, "Yet once more," indicates the removal of things that are shaken — that is, things that have been made — in order that the things that cannot be shaken may remain. 28 Therefore let us be grateful for receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, and thus let us offer to God acceptable worship, with reverence and awe, 29 for our God is a consuming fire.

Context

  • The author writes to a weary audience using the image of a race — believers with drooping arms and weak knees who need to keep running the Christian life to the end.
  • Esau serves as the cautionary example: a man who chose the path of least resistance, trading his future inheritance for a single meal of stew.

Passage summary

  • The precepts (vv. 12–17): Two commands — vigorously pursue peace with everyone, and see to it that no one in the community falls away.
  • The picture (vv. 18–24): A contrast between terrifying Mount Sinai (kept out) and welcoming Mount Zion (welcomed in) — the heavenly city where angels, the church, God, and Jesus gather in celebration.
  • The power (vv. 25–29): God will shake everything that can be shaken; only the unshakable kingdom remains — so let that reality fuel obedience.

Main points

Vigorously pursue peace — with everyone

Peace sounds simple but costs enormously, because the command extends even to people who don't want peace with you.

  • "Strive" carries the sense of intense, active pursuit — the same word used for pressing toward a goal in Philippians 3.
  • The command is not to achieve peace (that's not always in our hands) but to pursue it as far as we are able, as Romans 12 puts it.
  • Avoiding a difficult person is the path of least resistance — this passage gives no such out.

See to it that no one falls — pursue purity in community

Every member of the local church bears personal responsibility to make sure brothers and sisters finish the race.

  • "See to it" (v. 15) is the same shepherd language used in 1 Peter 5 — exercising oversight, going after those who wander.
  • Two dangers to guard against: a root of bitterness that defiles many, and spiritual unfaithfulness like Esau's — trading future inheritance for momentary ease.
  • There is no picture of passive Christianity here — you are someone else's mission, and they are yours.

We have come to Mount Zion — a better, unshakable kingdom

The contrast between Sinai (terror, exclusion) and Zion (celebration, welcome) shows how far the new covenant has brought us.

  • At Sinai, even touching the mountain meant death; at Zion, the unholy have been made righteous through the sprinkled blood of Jesus.
  • The heavenly Jerusalem is a great party — innumerable angels, the church enrolled as God's firstborn, God the judge, and Jesus the mediator.
  • God will shake the entire cosmos at the end; what remains is the kingdom — so live for what cannot be shaken.

The picture of home is what fuels the commands

You cannot simply command your heart to pursue peace or purity — you need the vision of the unshakable kingdom to sustain you when it costs.

  • Like the officer buried 22 hours under World Trade Center rubble who kept thinking of his family — "You kept me alive" — the vision of home gives power to endure.
  • The cost of pursuing peace cannot compare to what is ahead (Romans 8:18); the kingdom picture recalibrates what we're willing to pay.
  • When a frustrating brother or sister drains you, the picture reminds you: God calls them his firstborn — cherished more than we can imagine.

Key idea

You can't command your heart into holiness — you fill your soul with the realities of the kingdom, and that vision empowers you to pursue the peace and purity the race demands.

Discussion questions

1

Is there a person or situation where you've been taking the path of least resistance — avoiding pursuit of peace because it costs too much? What's one step toward pursuing it?

2

The sermon described community responsibility in striking terms: "You are our mission." Who in your church community are you actively watching over — and who might be slipping that nobody is noticing?

3

How vividly does the picture of the unshakable kingdom — Mount Zion, the celebration, home — actually shape your daily choices? What would it look like to fill your soul with that reality more intentionally?
